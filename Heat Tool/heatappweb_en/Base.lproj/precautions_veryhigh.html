Very High To Extreme Risk

Conditions are dangerous! Heat illness can develop faster and be more serious at this risk level. Extra measures are recommended.

Reschedule all non-essential work for days when the heat index is lower. Move essential work to the coolest part of the day – start earlier, split the work shift, or work evening/night shifts.

If emergency or essential work must be done at this heat index, then a designated knowledgeable person should be on-site to modify work activities and set work/rest schedules. STOP WORK if protective measures are not possible.

Important reminders (see OSHA’s heat illness campaign site for more information and resources):

Water and Shade

  • Drinking water must be on site. Establish a water drinking schedule (about 4 cups/hour).
  • Set up cool, shaded rest areas.

Emergency Planning and Response

  • Make sure medical help (clinic, hospital, emergency services) is nearby – if not, first aid must be on site.
  • Call 911 and cool a worker who is unconscious, confused, or uncoordinated. This may be heat stroke, which is a medical emergency.
  • Know where you are in case you need to call 911.

Work and Rest

  • Gradually build up the workload and allow more breaks over a week (acclimatize workers).
  • Establish and follow a work/rest schedule (amount of work time and rest time each work hour)
    • Take routine rest breaks in cool, shaded area
    • Remove protective clothing (if possible) while resting
    • Increase rest time as heat index rises or if workers show signs of heat illness
  • Adjust work activities
    • Do heavy tasks earlier in the day
    • Decrease the pace of heavy job tasks
    • Add more workers to heavy job tasks
    • Rotate workers between heavy and lighter jobs
  • Provide cooling measures (cool vests, cool mist stations)
  • Use more precautions for work in layers of protective clothing and the direct sun - see OSHA's heat illness campaign site
  • Use sunscreen and wear light-colored clothing.

Training

  • Alert all workers of extreme heat hazard.
  • Review heat-related illness topics before work begins
    • How to recognize heat-related illness
    • How to prevent it
    • What to do if someone gets sick

Watching Out for Heat Illness

  • Check heart rate, temperature, or other physical signs of heat exposure at rest breaks.
  • Watch out for each other.
  • Set up a buddy system.
  • Supervisors should maintain communications at all times with crew and watch for signs of heat illness.
